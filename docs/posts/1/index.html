On this page

  • Low-value care wastes money and distracts from true priorities

The current landscape of scientific literature is flooded with innovation. We now have AI models acing myocardial infarction ECG interpretation(Herman et al. 2023), an Amazon software that reads into your brain MRI and prepares a finalized report in Armenian (or just about any language you could possibly think of), and a plethora of prediction models which promise to personalize diagnosis and management of just about any body condition.

Do we actually need such abundance of progress? When focusing on cardiovascular disease and metabolic disorders, it appears that we don’t. Just validating and implementing prediction models that we already have available is challenging (Lucaroni et al. 2019) . We still fail to make patients take their blood pressure pills consistently(Burnier and Egan 2019), indulge in prescribing tests and treatments of dubious value(Kini et al. 2022); and we must do better than that.
